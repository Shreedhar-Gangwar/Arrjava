---
title: "The Three Pillars of a Temporary Injunction: Order XXXIX Before the U.P.
  Civil Courts"
category: Practice Note · Civil Procedure
date: 2026-06-01
abstract: Prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury are
  recited in every application and understood in few. A practitioner's note on
  pleading each pillar so the court can decide on a single reading.
---
Every application under Order XXXIX Rules 1 and 2 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, recites the same trinity: a prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury. The recital costs nothing; the demonstration is where applications succeed or fail.

#### Prima facie case is not a prima facie title

The applicant need not prove title at the interlocutory stage; the applicant must show a serious question to be tried and a credible foundation in the documents annexed. An application that argues the entire suit invites the court to prejudge it, and courts decline that invitation by dismissing the application.

#### Balance of convenience is comparative, not declaratory

The question is not whether the applicant suffers if the injunction is refused, but who suffers more, and whose suffering can later be compensated in money. The application should state, in one disciplined paragraph, what each party stands to lose in each scenario.

#### Irreparable injury must be pleaded as fact, not adjective

"Grave and irreparable loss" is a conclusion. The pleading must supply the fact that makes the loss irreparable: that the property is the applicant's sole residence, that demolition cannot be undone, and that possession once disturbed alters the subject matter of the suit itself.

The application that separates these three pillars, pleads each with facts, and closes with a precisely drawn prayer is the application that survives objection and earns the interim order.
